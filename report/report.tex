\begin{abstract}
We study whether youth slang normalization can be represented as a low-dimensional direction in embedding space.
Starting from a course-project proposal that hypothesized an ``Eigenslang'' vector,
we build a curated dataset of 96 slang--neutral pairs with context sentences and compare raw retrieval, a mean-offset baseline, and a PCA-based direction.
We evaluate two formulations of the hypothesis:
direct term/paraphrase offsets and context-preserving sentence offsets.
On our current dataset, the direct term/paraphrase formulation with sentence-transformer embeddings gives the best absolute retrieval performance,
while the contextual sentence formulation shows the only small positive gain from the PCA direction.
Overall, we find weak but not decisive evidence for a shared slang direction.
The main lesson is that representation choice and evaluation design matter substantially,
and that a single global direction is at best an incomplete explanation of slang normalization.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Modern NLP systems still struggle with recent youth slang.
Expressions such as \emph{mid}, \emph{cooked}, \emph{rizz}, \emph{ratioed}, and \emph{let him cook} are informal, fast-changing, and often highly context-dependent.
Even when a model has seen the relevant surface form, it may not represent the slang meaning accurately, especially when the same token also has a standard meaning.
This matters for text understanding, sentiment analysis, retrieval, and user-facing systems that interact with younger speakers.

Our project asks a simple geometric question:
is there a shared vector direction that maps a neutral expression toward its slang counterpart or, equivalently for normalization, maps slang toward a neutral paraphrase?
We call this candidate direction \emph{Eigenslang}.
Given slang--neutral pairs, we compute difference vectors and apply PCA to test whether the first principal component captures a meaningful shared transformation.

The project is intentionally modest.
We do not claim to solve slang normalization broadly.
Instead, we frame the contribution as a controlled empirical test of whether low-dimensional structure appears in slang--neutral differences.
This is a suitable course-project question because both positive and negative results are meaningful:
if the leading direction helps retrieval, that supports the hypothesis;
if not, that suggests slang may be too heterogeneous or context-specific for a single global offset.

Our main contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
\item a curated dataset of 96 slang examples with neutral targets and context;
\item two experimentally distinct formulations of the offset hypothesis, one proposal-faithful and one context-preserving;
\item a comparison of raw retrieval, mean-offset, and PCA-based Eigenslang baselines;
\item an analysis of what the current results do and do not support.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
Our work is motivated by three strands of literature.

First, prior work shows that slang benefits from specialized lexical resources and embeddings.
\citet{wilson2020urban} build Urban Dictionary-based embeddings for slang NLP and show that slang-specific lexical information can improve downstream behavior.
Similarly, social-media slang extraction work argues that non-standard language behaves differently from standard lexicons and often requires specialized treatment \citep{matsumoto2019slang}.

Second, our project is motivated by work on interpretable directions in embedding spaces.
\citet{shin2018eigenvectors} analyze eigenvectors of word-embedding spaces to uncover semantic structure.
More broadly, the idea that meaning can be organized along approximately linear directions is common in both classic embedding work and bias analysis \citep{bojanowski2017enriching, bolukbasi2016man}.
Our project adopts this style of reasoning, but targets slang normalization rather than demographic bias or classic analogy tasks.

Third, there is prior work on slang generation and transformation.
\citet{sun2021computational} propose a computational framework for slang generation, which is related to our project because both generation and normalization assume some systematic relation between standard and non-standard forms.
However, our problem is simpler and more controlled: we study retrieval-based normalization rather than full generative transformation.

For representations, our original proposal suggested starting with fastText because subword structure can help with rare or novel forms \citep{bojanowski2017enriching}.
In the current implementation, we instead compare TF--IDF and a sentence-transformer backend based on Sentence-BERT \citep{reimers2019sentence}, because many of our examples are phrase-level or context-dependent.
This design choice became especially important once we observed that isolated word-level paraphrases and full context sentences can behave differently.

\section{Dataset and Task}
\subsection{Dataset Construction}
We began with a small manually written dataset and then expanded it using a cleaned subset of the publicly available \texttt{MLBtrio/genz-slang-dataset} on Hugging Face \citep{mlbtrio2026dataset}.
The external dataset was used as a source pool rather than as trusted final data.
We imported raw rows, mapped them into our schema, filtered duplicates and low-quality entries, and selected external candidates conservatively.

The resulting cleaned dataset contains 96 examples:
\begin{itemize}
\item 60 manually written examples;
\item 36 selected external examples;
\item a final schema including slang term, context sentence, neutral expression, neutral paraphrase, and neutral sentence.
\end{itemize}

The dataset also stores category labels such as \texttt{judgment}, \texttt{emotion}, \texttt{online}, \texttt{intensifier}, and \texttt{dating\_social}.
These categories are currently somewhat coarse, but they are useful for later error analysis and for future improvements to candidate-set design.

\subsection{Why We Added Neutral Fields}
The original project idea only required a slang item, a context, and a neutral paraphrase.
During implementation, that turned out to be insufficient.
For example, a neutral paraphrase such as \emph{received far more criticism than support} is useful semantically, but direct string substitution can produce awkward neutralized sentences.
To support both proposal-faithful and context-preserving experiments, we introduced:
\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{neutral\_expression}: a short neutral word or phrase;
\item \texttt{neutral\_paraphrase}: a fuller descriptive gloss;
\item \texttt{neutral\_sentence}: an explicitly neutralized sentence.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Task Definition}
We formulate slang normalization as retrieval.
Given a slang-side representation, the system must retrieve the correct neutral target from a candidate set.
We report Top-1 and Top-3 retrieval accuracy.

Our current evaluation uses an all-candidate setup:
the correct neutral target is ranked against all candidate neutral labels from the dataset.
This setting is straightforward and reproducible, but it is also quite harsh.
We return to this limitation in \cref{sec:discussion}.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Two Offset Formulations}
Our proposal described the basic difference vector as
\[
\mathbf{d}_i = \mathbf{s}_i - \mathbf{n}_i,
\]
where $\mathbf{s}_i$ is a slang embedding and $\mathbf{n}_i$ is the embedding of its neutral paraphrase.

In implementation, we evaluate two versions of this idea.

\paragraph{Term/paraphrase offset.}
This is the proposal-faithful formulation:
\[
\mathbf{d}_i =
\operatorname{Embed}(\text{slang term})
-
\operatorname{Embed}(\text{neutral expression}).
\]
This formulation is simple and directly tests whether slang and neutral expressions lie along a common offset in embedding space.

\paragraph{Contextual sentence offset.}
Because many slang items are polysemous or phrase-level, we also evaluate
\[
\mathbf{d}_i =
\operatorname{Embed}(\text{slang sentence})
-
\operatorname{Embed}(\text{neutral sentence}).
\]
For example, the standard and slang meanings of \emph{ate}, \emph{cooked}, and \emph{serve} can differ substantially.
This contextual formulation attempts to preserve the intended meaning in a way that isolated words may not.

\subsection{Embedding Backends}
We compare two embedding backends:
\begin{itemize}
\item TF--IDF, as a simple lexical baseline;
\item a sentence-transformer backend using \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} \citep{reimers2019sentence}.
\end{itemize}

TF--IDF is not expected to capture slang semantics well, but it is useful as a sanity check.
The sentence-transformer is a stronger semantic baseline and supports both phrase-level and sentence-level inputs.

\subsection{Baselines and Eigenslang}
For each training example we compute a slang--neutral difference vector.
From these differences we derive three methods:

\paragraph{Raw retrieval.}
We compare the slang-side representation directly to all neutral candidates using cosine similarity.

\paragraph{Mean offset.}
We compute the mean training difference vector
\[
\bar{\mathbf{d}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_i \mathbf{d}_i
\]
and apply
\[
\mathbf{s}' = \mathbf{s} - \alpha \bar{\mathbf{d}}.
\]

\paragraph{PCA Eigenslang.}
We perform PCA on the training difference vectors and take the first principal component $\mathbf{e}_1$ as the candidate Eigenslang direction.
We then apply
\[
\mathbf{s}' = \mathbf{s} - \alpha \mathbf{e}_1.
\]
The scaling factor $\alpha$ is tuned on validation data.

\section{Experiments}
\subsection{Setup}
We split the cleaned dataset into train, validation, and test sets with a deterministic 60/20/20 split.
For each split, we evaluate raw retrieval, mean offset, and PCA Eigenslang under both representation modes and both embedding backends.

The current experiment is therefore a four-way representation/embedding comparison:
\begin{itemize}
\item TF--IDF + contextual sentence;
\item TF--IDF + term/paraphrase;
\item sentence-transformer + contextual sentence;
\item sentence-transformer + term/paraphrase.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{What the Results Suggest}
Three points matter most.

First, TF--IDF is clearly too weak for this task.
This is not surprising, since many of the examples depend on semantic nuance rather than surface-level lexical overlap.

Second, the proposal-faithful term/paraphrase formulation with a sentence-transformer backend gives the best absolute performance.
Its test Top-1 accuracy is 0.10 and Top-3 accuracy is 0.25.
However, PCA Eigenslang does not improve over raw retrieval in this setting.

Third, the contextual sentence formulation shows the only small positive effect from PCA Eigenslang.
With the sentence-transformer backend, raw retrieval achieves test Top-1 = 0.00 and Top-3 = 0.05, while PCA Eigenslang improves this to test Top-1 = 0.05 and Top-3 = 0.10.
This is a weak signal, but it is the closest thing we currently have to positive evidence for a shared direction.

\subsection{Qualitative Interpretation}
The current results suggest that representation choice matters as much as, or more than, the PCA hypothesis itself.
The term/paraphrase representation is better for absolute retrieval, likely because it is closer to the actual label space.
The contextual representation is noisier, but it may also be the only place where a shared sentence-level shift is visible.

One possible interpretation is that slangness is not a single universal property at the lexical level, but that context-specific transformations may still have partial low-dimensional structure.
Another interpretation is that the current all-candidate retrieval task is too difficult and obscures real effects.

\section{Discussion and Future Work}\label{sec:discussion}
Our current conclusion is cautious.
We do \emph{not} have strong evidence that a robust, universal Eigenslang direction exists.
The direct term/paraphrase formulation performs best overall, but PCA does not improve it.
The contextual sentence formulation produces a small PCA gain, but the absolute numbers remain low.

This still constitutes a meaningful course project result.
The project now demonstrates:
\begin{itemize}
\item a working end-to-end experimental pipeline;
\item a curated dataset with explicit neutral-side structure;
\item a comparison between two formulations of the offset hypothesis;
\item a controlled negative-to-weakly-positive empirical result.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Strengths.}
The strongest part of the project is methodological clarity.
We started from a proposal-faithful hypothesis, discovered a genuine implementation issue caused by polysemy and phrase-level slang, and turned that into an explicit comparison between term/paraphrase and contextual sentence offsets.
This makes the project more rigorous than if we had silently drifted from the proposal.

\paragraph{Weaknesses.}
The main weakness is the evaluation design.
Our all-candidate retrieval setup is difficult and likely too noisy.
The test set is also still relatively small, even after dataset cleaning and expansion.
Finally, some neutral sentences are still only approximately natural, and category labels are somewhat coarse.

\paragraph{What we would do next.}
With more time, the next three steps would be:
\begin{itemize}
\item add category-restricted or balanced-distractor evaluation settings;
\item analyze PCA more directly, for example by reporting variance explained by the first few components;
\item further refine the clean dataset and expand it to roughly 100--120 examples with more balanced categories.
\end{itemize}

We would also consider a fastText baseline, as originally proposed, because subword information could be useful for rare or morphologically novel slang.
At the same time, our current results suggest that improving evaluation design is likely more important than adding yet another embedding model.

\section{Conclusion}
We tested whether youth slang normalization has a shared low-dimensional structure in embedding space.
Using a cleaned 96-example dataset, we compared TF--IDF and sentence-transformer embeddings, raw retrieval, mean-offset baselines, and PCA-based Eigenslang directions under both direct term/paraphrase and contextual sentence formulations.

The current evidence is mixed.
The term/paraphrase formulation performs better overall, but PCA does not help it.
The contextual formulation shows a small PCA gain, but its absolute retrieval accuracy remains low.
Thus, our best current conclusion is that a single global Eigenslang direction is at most a weak and representation-dependent phenomenon.
This is still informative: it suggests that slang normalization may require more structured evaluation and perhaps richer context-sensitive modeling than a single shared offset can provide.
